# Glossary — Parental Alienation Terms

The vocabulary you'll see in court papers, clinical reports, academic articles, and forum threads. Definitions written for parents and non-specialist lawyers — with notes on how each term is actually used (and sometimes misused).

---

## Core terminology

### Parental Alienation (PA)
A pattern in which one parent acts — through words, behaviour, or restriction of contact — to damage the child's relationship with the other parent. Distinct from a child's *organic* rejection of a parent for legitimate reasons (abuse, neglect). PA describes the *behaviour*; the child's resulting state is *alienation*.

### Parental Alienation Syndrome (PAS)
Term coined by Richard Gardner (1985) for the cluster of symptoms in a child who has been alienated. **Use with caution in court** — PAS is not in the DSM-5 and many judges flinch from the term. Most modern practitioners argue *behaviours* and *resist-refuse dynamics* rather than diagnose a *syndrome*.

### Resist-Refuse Dynamics (RRD)
Joan Kelly & Janet Johnston's (2001) framework. A child's resistance or refusal to see a parent falls on a spectrum with multiple possible causes — alienation is one, but so are realistic estrangement (justified by parent's actual conduct), affinity (developmentally normal closeness to one parent), enmeshment, and others. Sophisticated evaluators use RRD rather than a binary "alienated / not alienated" framing.

### Alienating Behaviours
Specific acts that can produce alienation. Baker & Fine's 17-behaviour list is the most cited:
1. Bad-mouthing the other parent
2. Limiting contact
3. Erasing the other parent from the child's life
4. Forcing the child to choose
5. Telling the child the other parent doesn't love them
6. Confiding adult information
7. Withdrawing love if the child shows positive feelings toward the other parent
8. Telling the child the other parent is dangerous
9. Forcing the child to reject the other parent
10. Asking the child to spy on the other parent
11. Asking the child to keep secrets from the other parent
12. Referring to the other parent by first name
13. Referring to a step-parent as "mum"/"dad" and encouraging the child to do the same
14. Withholding medical/academic/social information
15. Changing the child's surname
16. Cultivating dependency
17. Undermining the other parent's authority

### Targeted Parent
The parent against whom alienating behaviours are directed and from whom the child is being distanced. Sometimes called the "rejected" or "non-favoured" parent.

### Alienating Parent
The parent engaging in alienating behaviours. Sometimes called the "favoured" or "preferred" parent. Note: "favoured" describes the child's current expressed preference, not the parent's actual quality.

### Hybrid Cases
Cases where both alienating behaviours *and* legitimate estrangement factors are present. Most cases are not pure. Hybrid cases require nuanced intervention.

## Clinical / diagnostic terms

### DSM-5
The American Psychiatric Association's *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th edition*. **PA is not a named diagnosis in DSM-5** but related codes can apply:
- V61.20: Parent-Child Relational Problem
- V61.29: Child Affected by Parental Relationship Distress
- V995.51: Child Psychological Abuse

### ICD-11
The World Health Organisation's *International Classification of Diseases, 11th revision*. ICD-11 includes the code **QE52.0: Caregiver-child relationship problem**, which can be applied to PA-pattern cases. Active debate continues over more specific recognition.

### Mild / Moderate / Severe alienation
Warshak's severity framework:
- **Mild**: child resists transitions but maintains relationship once with targeted parent
- **Moderate**: child resists contact and shows hostility but warms over a visit
- **Severe**: child refuses contact entirely; sustained hostility; immune to evidence

Each tier calls for different intervention intensity.

### Independent Thinker Phenomenon
A clinical marker of alienation. The child insists their negative views of the targeted parent are entirely their own, refusing to acknowledge any influence from the favoured parent — often using the favoured parent's exact vocabulary.

### Borrowed Scenarios
The alienated child describes events or behaviours from the targeted parent that they cannot personally have witnessed — often events from before the child's birth or out of their presence. A diagnostic clue.

### Lack of Ambivalence
Normal child relationships are ambivalent (love + frustration). The alienated child describes the targeted parent in absolutes (all bad). The favoured parent in absolutes (all good). The absence of normal ambivalence is itself a clinical sign.

## Legal terms (jurisdiction-dependent)

### Custody / Residence / Parental Responsibility
The same concept under different labels:
- **US**: "Custody" (physical and legal)
- **UK**: "Residence" + "Parental Responsibility"
- **Australia**: "Parental responsibility" + "Lives-with order"
- **EU civil-law systems**: variations on "custody" + "parental authority"

### Child Arrangements Order
UK Children Act 1989 term for the post-2014 replacement of separate "contact" and "residence" orders. Covers where the child lives and with whom they spend time.

### Best Interests of the Child
The overriding statutory principle in most family-law jurisdictions. Specific factor lists vary — UK's "welfare checklist" (CA 1989 s.1(3)), most US states use a similar list, etc.

### Article 8 ECHR
Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights — right to respect for private and family life. Frequently argued in PA cases at the European Court of Human Rights (ECtHR) when domestic family courts have failed to protect a parent-child relationship.

### Section 91(14) Order
UK provision (CA 1989 s.91(14)) restricting future applications without permission of the court. Sometimes used against alienating parents engaging in vexatious applications, sometimes against targeted parents to limit "harassment" — context-dependent.

### CAFCASS / Cafcass Cymru / Children's Guardian
England & Wales — Children and Family Court Advisory and Support Service. The body of social workers and family-court advisers who assess children and report to the court. Cafcass involvement is standard in contested family-court cases.

### Section 7 Report
UK term for a welfare report ordered by the court, typically prepared by Cafcass or the local authority. Usually decisive in contested cases.

### Forensic Evaluation / Custody Evaluation
US/Canadian term for the equivalent: a court-appointed mental-health professional assesses the family and makes recommendations.

### Reunification Therapy
Specialised family therapy aimed at restoring a damaged parent-child relationship after alienation. Distinct from general family counselling. Programs include Family Bridges (Warshak), Family Reflections (Childress), Overcoming Barriers Family Camp, Transitioning Families.

### Hague Convention (1980)
The Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction. Provides for prompt return of children wrongfully removed across borders. [See cross-border playbook.](../playbooks/cross-border-hague.md)

### Brussels II-ter (EU Regulation 2019/1111)
EU regulation governing cross-border family matters (custody, abduction) within EU member states. Operational from 1 August 2022, replacing Brussels IIa.

### Article 13(b) defence
The "grave risk" defence under the Hague Convention — argument that returning the child to the country of habitual residence would expose them to physical or psychological harm. Most-litigated Hague defence.

## Forensic / evaluation terms

### Collateral Information
Reports from third parties — teachers, doctors, family members — that an evaluator gathers to triangulate parent accounts.

### Psychological Testing Battery
A standardised set of tests an evaluator administers. May include MMPI-3, MCMI-IV, Rorschach, parenting-stress measures. Results interpret in context, not as standalone diagnoses.

### Confirmation Bias (in evaluators)
The risk that an evaluator who frames a case as "alienation" reads all evidence to support that framing — or the reverse. Why courts increasingly require evaluators trained in *both* PA dynamics and domestic-violence assessment.

### Court-Appointed Special Advocate (CASA) — US
A trained volunteer assigned to represent a child's best interests in court. Quality varies widely.

## Communication & strategy terms

### BIFF
Bill Eddy's method for high-conflict communication: **B**rief, **I**nformative, **F**riendly, **F**irm. Reduces escalation in co-parenting messages and produces a defensible written record.

### Grey-Rock
A communication strategy of becoming emotionally unreactive (like a grey rock) to a high-conflict ex's provocations. Drawn from narcissistic-abuse literature, controversial in family-court contexts.

### Yellow-Rock
A more functional variant: respond minimally but politely, maintaining communication on essentials while emotionally disengaging.

### Parallel Parenting
Co-parenting model for high-conflict cases. Each parent makes decisions independently during their parenting time, with minimal coordination. Reduces conflict but requires structural separation of responsibilities.

## Statistical / epidemiological terms

### Prevalence
What proportion of separated families experience PA. Harman et al. estimates run 11–15% of US adults reporting having experienced PA in childhood. International figures vary by methodology.

### Gender symmetry
PA occurs roughly evenly across mother- and father-targeted patterns when prevalence is measured directly (Harman, Lorandos). Family-court samples skew toward father-targeted because of who comes to court — beware reading court samples as prevalence.

## Terms to use with care in court

| Term | Why caution |
|---|---|
| "PAS" | Not in DSM-5; many judges dismiss the framing |
| "Brainwashing" | Sounds inflammatory; "alienating behaviours" is cleaner |
| "Munchausen by proxy" / "DARVO" | Unless an expert is presenting it, parents using these terms read as armchair-diagnosis |
| "Toxic" / "narcissist" | Sounds editorial; describe specific behaviours instead |

| Term | Better alternative |
|---|---|
| "PAS" | "Alienating behaviours" or "resist-refuse dynamics" |
| "Brainwashing" | "Coaching" or "shaping the child's narrative" |
| "Always" / "Never" | "On [N] documented occasions…" |
