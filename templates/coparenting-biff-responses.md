# Coparenting Communication — BIFF Method Canned Responses

When every message from the other parent is an attempt to provoke a reaction that will end up in their court bundle as evidence of your instability, you need a response style that:

- Doesn't engage the provocation
- Doesn't escalate
- Doesn't lose you the moral high ground
- Doesn't create evidence against you

**BIFF** = **B**rief, **I**nformative, **F**riendly, **F**irm. (Bill Eddy, *BIFF: Quick Responses to High-Conflict People*.)

## The BIFF rules

- **Brief** — three sentences max where possible.
- **Informative** — about the topic at hand, with facts.
- **Friendly** — neutral pleasant tone, never sarcastic, never cold.
- **Firm** — close the loop. Don't invite further argument.

## Canned response patterns

### When they cancel a contact slot at short notice

> "Understood — sorry [child] isn't well. I'll plan to see them on the next scheduled day. Let me know if there's anything I can drop off in the meantime."

### When they make a vague accusation

> "I want to make sure I understand. Could you let me know the specific date and incident you're referring to? I'll respond as soon as I have the details."

### When they refuse to discuss the parenting plan

> "Happy to discuss next week if that works better. In the meantime, the schedule under the [date of order] order still applies."

### When they propose a change that disadvantages your contact

> "Thanks for raising this. I want to give it proper thought. Can you put the proposal in writing with the proposed dates? I'll respond by [date]."

### When they accuse you of something

> "I don't see it that way, but I'd rather not get into a back-and-forth by text. If it's important, please raise it through [mediator / lawyer / parenting coordinator]."

### When they ask for an immediate decision

> "I'll get back to you by [end of day / tomorrow morning]."

### When they send a long inflammatory message

> "I've read your message. I'll respond to the practical points once I've had time to consider them."

## What never to write back

- "You always…" / "You never…"
- "The court will see this for what it is."
- Any reference to their new partner, their family, their finances, their character.
- Any emoji.
- Any "fixed!" / "lol" / "??" / "wow."
- Any all-caps.
- Anything you wouldn't be comfortable reading aloud in court.

## How to actually maintain the discipline

The hardest part isn't writing BIFF responses — it's not sending the first draft of the angry response.

Practice: **draft → wait two hours → re-read as if the judge will see it → send the shortest version that closes the loop.**

If the message you're drafting makes you feel powerful while you write it, it's the wrong message.

## Storing the exchange

Every message in / out goes into your contact log's `Source` column. Screenshot or export the thread monthly so cloud deletions can't lose it.
